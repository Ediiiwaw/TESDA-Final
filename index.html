Welcome to the Philippines

The Philippines is an archipelago in Southeast Asia consisting of more than 7,000 islands, with a population of over 100 million people. It has a rich cultural heritage influenced by various factors including indigenous traditions, Spanish colonization, American occupation, and interactions with neighboring Asian countries.

Pambansang Watawat

The national flag of the Philippines features a horizontal bicolor design with blue on top, symbolizing peace, truth, and justice, and red on the bottom, representing patriotism and valor. A white equilateral triangle at the hoist stands for equality and fraternity. In the center of the triangle is a golden-yellow sun with eight primary rays, each representing a province pivotal to the 1896 Philippine Revolution against Spanish rule. Each corner of the triangle contains a five-pointed golden-yellow star, symbolizing the three major island groups: Luzon, Visayas (originally referred as Panay Island), and Mindanao. The flag is a powerful symbol of the nation's independence, sovereignty, and unity, prominently displayed during national holidays and important events.

Pambansang Awit

The national anthem of the Philippines, "Lupang Hinirang," translates to "Chosen Land." It is a patriotic song expressing love and devotion to the country, played during national events and ceremonies. The music was composed by Julian Felipe in 1898, and the original Spanish lyrics were written by José Palma. The anthem was first performed during the proclamation of Philippine independence on June 12, 1898. The lyrics were later translated into Filipino and English. It is played during national events, ceremonies, and important gatherings, serving as a reminder of the country's struggle for independence and its enduring spirit of freedom.

Pambansang Hayop

The carabao has been a symbol of hard work, resilience, and the agricultural heritage of the Philippines for centuries. Its image is frequently used in various national symbols, emblems, and cultural representations. The term "Pambansang Kalabaw" is sometimes used metaphorically to refer to the Filipino people's resilience and dedication to hard work, especially in the face of challenges.

Pambansang Isda

Bangus is the national fish of Philippines, called milkfish in English. It is an adaptable, tough, and sturdy fish that can survive in confined spaces, so it is regularly bred on fish farms, which is the reason why it is widely available all over the Philippines. The flavor of milkfish is distinctive and mild, best paired with complementary ingredients. Bangus is usually cooked in soups, fried, grilled, barbecued, stuffed or stewed in various spices, ginger and vinegar, but the varieties of bangus-based dishes are numerous.

Pambansang Ibon

The Philippine Eagle was named the national bird of the Philippines. The Philippine Eagle, as its name suggests, is found only in the Philippines, an island country in Southeast Asia. While the Philippines consists of more than 7,000 islands, the Philippine Eagle is found on only four of them, all within the northern and eastern regions of the country. It is one of the rarest birds in the world, with an estimated population of fewer than than 500 individuals. It also symbolizes the rick biodiversity of the Philippines and the nation's commitment to wildlife conversation.

Pambansang Puno

The Narra Tree, or Pterocarpus indicus, is the official and symbolic tree of the Philippines. It is revered for its strength, resilience, and cultural significance. The Narra Tree’s unique features and beauty make it a cherished part of Filipino culture and art. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect this national treasure. The Narra Tree’s designation as the Philippine national tree highlights its importance as a symbol of national identity and natural heritage.

Pambansang Bulaklak

The Sampaguita (Jasminum sambac), known for its small, white, and fragrant flowers often used in garlands and offerings, was declared the national flower of the Philippines in 1934 by Governor General Frank Murphy. It symbolizes purity, simplicity, and humility—virtues highly esteemed in Filipino culture. The flowers are notable for their strong, sweet fragrance, which is most intense at night when the flowers bloom​
